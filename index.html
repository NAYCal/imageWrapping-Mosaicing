Mosaic

In order to form the mosaic, both images have to be aligned and facing the same direction. I approached this problem by first warping the left image and its corresponding points. Then, I took the minimum x, y values from the corresponding points in both warped and right image to find the distance necessary to align both images. Since the corresponding points are unique and the minimum x, y values do not change due to the change in perspective, this worked. Afterwards, I calculated a gradient mask to apply onto the images such that I could safely combine both images and achieve a seemless addition.

Problems Encountered

In this project, I had faced a major problem. Although my initial warping function had produced a similar output as that of OpenCV’s warpPerspective(), the results were not ideal. This was because the warp had cut off areas of the image that would have not been in the image plane I am warping onto, resulting a massive loss of visual data.

Lessons Learned

The most significant lesson learned in this project for me was to choose implementations that would be easier to debug. Although this was not mentioned in the Problems Encountered section, the problems faced would have been avoided had I chose to use inverse warping sooner. I had picked forward warping initially because of its straight forward implementation. However, when it came to debugging and drawing things out, it became harder as it was easier for me to think from the warped image to the original image, rather than from the original image to the warped. Therefore, I should take approaches that would be easier to dissect rather than just implement.
